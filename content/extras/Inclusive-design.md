<h1 class="post-title-only">Inclusive Design</h1>

Inclusive design is a approach that aims to make products, services, and content usable and welcoming for as many different people as possible, across ages, backgrounds, abilities, and contexts. 

It goes beyond minimum accessibility compliance to actively look for who might be excluded and to remove or reduce those barriers.

In practice, this means we:

- Assume diversity as the default, not the edge case.
- Involve a range of people in research and testing, especially those who are often excluded.
- Consider different devices, connection speeds, environments, and levels of comfort with technology.

Inclusive design is an ongoing mindset, not a one‑off checklist.



## Why it matters

Designing inclusively strengthens brands by making it easier for more people to find, use, and trust what we offer. It supports our ethical commitments, reduces legal and reputational risk, and often improves the experience for everyone, not just people with specific access needs.

Inclusive design means:

- Our content is readable, understandable, and respectful.
- Our interfaces are clear, forgiving of error, and work across a range of devices and capabilities.
- Our choices (visuals, language, interactions) avoid stereotyping and unnecessary exclusion.



## Core principles

We use established inclusive design and accessibility principles as a baseline. In practical terms, this includes:

- **Equity**: People with different abilities or contexts can complete key tasks with similar ease and dignity.
- **Flexibility**: Interfaces work with different input methods, preferences, and assistive technologies.
- **Clarity**: Content and controls are simple, well‑structured, and easy to perceive (e.g., good contrast, clear hierarchy, plain language).
- **Tolerance for error**: The experience helps people avoid mistakes and recover easily when they happen.
- **Participation**: We seek feedback from a diverse range of users and iterate based on what we learn.

Accessibility standards (such as WCAG) are treated as a minimum technical baseline, not the full definition of inclusion.



## How website owners apply this

When creating or updating content or features, website owners should:

- Write in clear, concise language and explain jargon when it’s unavoidable.
- Use meaningful headings, lists, and descriptive link text (e.g., “Download pricing guide” instead of “Click here”).
- Ensure images have alt text that conveys their purpose, or are marked decorative if purely visual.
- Avoid relying on color alone to convey meaning and maintain sufficient color contrast.
- Check that all key actions can be completed with a keyboard and are announced clearly to assistive technologies.
- Choose photography, illustrations, and examples that reflect a variety of people and situations, without tokenism.
