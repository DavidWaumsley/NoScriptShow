<h1 class="post-title-only">Website Copy </h1>

Website copy explains what you do, who it is for, and what visitors can do next. It includes headlines, body text, calls‑to‑action, product or service descriptions, and supporting content such as FAQs or testimonials.

Good website copy helps visitors understand quickly, feel confident they are in the right place, and take a clear next step that suits both their needs and the business goals.


## Why it matters

Most visitors decide within a few seconds whether to stay or leave a page, and unclear or generic copy is a common reason people bounce. Strong website copy supports our brand positioning, improves conversion, and reduces support burden by answering common questions early and clearly.

**Good web copy means:**

- People can quickly grasp what we offer and whether it is for them.
- The most important actions are obvious and feel low‑risk to take.
- We sound consistent across pages, channels, and over time.



## Core principles we follow

We treat website copy as part of the overall user experience, not just “marketing text”. In practice, this means:

- **Audience first**: We write for specific people and their problems, not for “everyone” or for internal stakeholders.
- **One main purpose per page**: Each key page has a clear primary goal (e.g., “enquire”, “book a call”, “learn if this is right for you”).
- **Benefits before features**: We explain how we help before listing technical details or internal processes.
- **Plain, concrete language**: We avoid jargon and vague claims, using simple words and specific examples instead.
- **Scannable structure**: We use clear headings, short paragraphs, and lists so people can scan and still understand.
- **Credibility signals**: We support claims with proof such as testimonials, case studies, or simple numbers where appropriate.



## How website owners apply this

When creating or editing pages, website owners can follow these practical tips:

- **Start with the visitor’s question**
Before writing, identify the main question or doubt a visitor brings to this page (for example, “Is this for me?” or “What does it cost?”). Answers these early and clearly.
- **Write clear, outcome‑focused headlines**
Use headlines that say what someone will get or learn, not just internal labels.
- **Use the words your audience uses**
Mirror real customer language from emails, chats, reviews, and search terms rather than inventing new terminology. This improves clarity and search performance at the same time.
- **Make pages easy to scan**
Break content into sections with meaningful sub‑headings, use bullet lists for key points, and keep paragraphs short (one idea per paragraph). Put the most important information near the top “above the fold” where it is likely to be seen first.
- **Write specific calls‑to‑action (CTAs)**
Use CTAs that say exactly what will happen next (for example, “Book a 15‑minute call” or “Download price guide”) rather than vague phrases like “Submit” or “Click here”.
- **Cut ruthlessly**
Remove filler phrases, repeated points, and internal detail that visitors do not need to make a decision. Shorter, cleaner pages generally perform better than long, unfocused ones.
- **Review regularly**
Schedule simple reviews of key pages to check for out‑of‑date information, broken promises, or content that no longer matches how the business actually works.
