<h1 class="post-title-only">Content Design</h1>

## What we mean by content design

Content design is a discipline created by Sarah Richards (now Winters) while transforming GOV.UK from hundreds of disconnected government websites into a single, user‑centred resource. 

In her own words, it is about "using data and evidence to give the audience what they need, at the time they need it, and in a way they expect."[^1] [^2]


**It's a different way of thinking about content:**

- Content is not written first and designed second — both happen together, shaped by evidence.
- The format (words, video, a tool, a table, nothing at all) is a design decision, not a default.[^3]
- The goal is to meet a genuine user need, not to fill a page or promote the organisation.[^4]

The approach has been adopted well beyond government. Meta (Facebook) formally switched its content strategy teams to content design, embedding content designers across every product team.  Companies like Shopify, Atlassian, and Apple have followed.[^5] [^6] [^7] [^8]



## Why it matters

Most websites are written from the inside out — reflecting what the organisation wants to say. Content design flips that. It starts with what users are actually trying to do and works backwards.[^9] [^1]

**Content design means:**

- We create content because there is a real user need for it, not because it feels like something we should have.
- We choose the right format for the job, whether that is a short paragraph, a list, a video, or a single direct answer.
- We treat content as a live thing that can be improved, pruned, or removed when it stops serving users.[^10] [^9]

This aligns directly with our agile approach: ship useful content, measure it, and evolve it.

***

## Core principles we follow

These principles sit at the heart of good content design:[^1][^4][^9][^10]

- **User needs first**: Every piece of content should answer a real question or help a user complete a real task. If it doesn't, it probably shouldn't exist.
- **Evidence over assumption**: Decisions about what to publish and how are based on data, research, and audience language — not internal preference or habit.[^11] [^1]
- **Format follows need**: Words are not always the answer. The right format might be a table, a checklist, a visual, or a very short sentence.
- **Plain language**: Content uses the words the audience actually uses, not jargon, brand language, or insider terminology.[^12][^4]
- **Trust through clarity**: Unclear or unreliable content erodes trust. Consistency, accuracy, and honesty are non‑negotiable.[^4]
- **Content has a lifecycle**: All content should be planned, reviewed, and retired — not just published and forgotten.[^9]

***

## How website owners apply this

Content design is not just for specialists. Website owners can apply this thinking every time they add or update content:

- Before writing anything, ask: what does the user actually need to know or do here?
- Look for evidence — search queries, questions customers ask, analytics on what people click or ignore.
- Write in plain language; avoid jargon, use short sentences, and structure content with meaningful headings.
- Choose the simplest format that meets the need. If a list works better than prose, use a list.
- Remove or update content that is out of date, rarely visited, or no longer relevant.
- When in doubt, cut. Shorter, clearer content almost always outperforms longer, denser content.[^12][^4]
