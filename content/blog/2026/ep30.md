## What Is Content Design?

- Is a discipline created by Sarah Richards (now Winters) while transforming
GOV.UK from hundreds government websites into a single resource.
- Has been adopted by other governments such as the US, Australia, and New Zealand.
- Meta (Facebook) formally switched to content design, embedding content designers across every product team.
- Companies like Shopify, Atlassian, Apple, Google, and Microsoft have followed.

## A Different Way of Thinking

- Content is not written first and designed second — both happen together, shaped by evidence.
- The format (words, video, a tool, a table, nothing at all) is a design decision, not a default.
- The goal is to meet a genuine user need, not to fill a page or promote the organisation.

## Why It Matters

Most websites are written inside out - reflecting what the organisation wants to say.
Content design flips that and starts with what users are actually trying to do. So...


- We create content because there is a real user need for it, not because it feels like something we should have.
- We choose the right format for the job, whether that is a short paragraph, a list, a video, or a single direct answer.
- We treat content as a live thing that can be improved, pruned, or removed when it stops serving users.

## Core Principles

- All content should answer a real question or help complete a task.
- Decisions are based on research and audience — not internal preference.
- Words are not always the answer — consider tables, checklists, and visuals.
- Use words the audience uses, not jargon, brand, or insider terminology.
- Unclear content erodes trust — be consistent, accurate, and honest.
- Content should be planned, reviewed, and retired — not forgotten.

## How Website Owners Apply This
 
- Look for evidence — search queries, customer questions, or analytics.
- Use plain language; avoid jargon and provide meaningful headings.
- Choose the simplest format — if a list works better than prose, use a list.
- Remove or update content that is rarely visited or no longer relevant.
- When in doubt, cut — short and clear tends to perform better.
