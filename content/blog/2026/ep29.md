# The Website Owner's Manual

A customizable guide for creating an effective brand identity

## Slide 1: Title Slide

### The Website Owner's Manual


#### Notes:

The Website Owner's Manual is platform-agnostic.

Canva is used because:
1. It saves to PPTX or PDF for use with PowerPoint or LibreOffice.
2. Its real-time collaboration and commenting system allows multiple users to edit simultaneously.
3. Its whiteboard allows one place to store assets and experiment.
4. It's popular with small businesses (over 230 million monthly active users).
5. You get one month's free access to the pro version with vast stock resources.
6. Advanced presentation tools like audience Q&A, remote control, and drawing on slides.

---

As image editing software, it's limited compared to Figma, Adobe, or Canva's own Affinity Studio, but professional collaborators can add their work to Canva.

## Slide 2: Preface

### Principles



#### Web Principles
- Fluid before fixed units
- Mobile before desktop
- Semantics before styling
- Performance before bloat
- Accessibility before complexity

#### Business Principles
- Problem before product
- Justification before dependencies
- Local skills before global corporate platforms
- Simplicity before automation

#### Design Principles
- Content before design
- User needs before designer/stakeholder ego
- Objectives before personal taste

### About This Manual

The Website Owner's Manual is a project management tool. A collaborative hub for strategic planning and brand assets.

Like Paul Boag's book of the same name, it aims to give owners control over the legal, technical, and business responsibilities that they inherit with website ownership.

It encourages independence and "Inclusive Design" through Web Standards, Agile development and the IndieWeb.

#### Notes:

Designers can use this to remind clients of the unique features of the web, their responsibilities, and head off potential friction.

For example:
1. It makes it easier to see what and when a platform is needed, rather than picking it for them and subsequently having to justify it.
2. It provides the tools to assess other sites, avoiding telling the owner their ideas are bad and having to criticize the work of others.
3. It positions design as problem-solving to avoid the process becoming a community art project.
4. Reduces the need for torturous contracts.

---

Paul Boag's 2009 O'Reilly book is now available for free. It is still relevant, but predates the general shift to Agile development processes.

## Slide 4: Foundation & Strategy

### Who are you and why should anyone care?



- **Competitor Analysis** - Competitors' strengths and weaknesses. Differentiation opportunities.

- **Brand Purpose & Vision** - Why the business exists. Emotions it invokes. Core values and aims.

- **Target Audience** - Personas. Demographics. Visitor behaviour and their challenges.

- **Market Position Statement** - Where you sit and what you stand for. Your elevator pitch.

- **SEO Strategy** - Sanity checking achievable traffic. Content gaps. Keyword competition. Search intent. EEAT.

#### Notes:

This is often the hardest part.

Creating a brand requires introspection, and many in business are people of action who employ their gut to adapt to situations.

Online, we have to define upfront a purpose and target audience to cut through the noise and avoid being for no-one in particular.

Online, customers can't meet you first, so your brand identity has to act as your "digital handshake".

On the plus side, this work can act as a useful "North Star" for general business decisions and reduce the need to compete solely on price.

---

## Slide 5: Content & Voice Guidelines

### How you should communicate



- **Brand Voice** - Tone. Language style. Personality.

- **Writing Guidelines** - Best practices for web copy. SEO structure. Meta description templates. Alt text standards.

- **Content Types** - Blog posts. Landing pages. Social media. Directories. Flyers.

#### Notes:

People buy from people, so we have to cut through the impersonal nature of the web.

It's not "creative writing", but reputation control.

It tells people what they should expect in advance.

---

## Slide 6: Visual Identity

### Creating an emotional connection



- **Logo and Favicon** - Dark/light modes. Stacked/horizontal. Favicon. Open Graph images.

- **Typography** - Responsive type scale (mobile → desktop). Line height. Letter spacing for screens. Title and sentence case rules.

- **Fonts** - Font stacks with fallbacks. Height, letter spacing. Loading strategy and performance impact.

- **Colours** - Colour values. Light mode and dark modes. Hover and focus states. WCAG contrast ratios.

#### Notes:

This should be congruent with the brand's written voice and mission.

We should choose based on what objectively best represents the brand to users.

Remember that some visitors will only see or hear your written content (screen readers and search bots).

Increasingly, some will have preferences that will remove a website's styling (high-contrast themes and monochrome screens).

---

## Slide 7: Image & Media Guidelines

### Making content easy and enjoyable to consume



- **Image Specifications** - File formats. Fallbacks? Responsive image sizes. Aspect ratios for different contexts.

- **Video & Audio Guidelines** - Aspect ratios. Length. Hosting. Auto-play policies. Transcripts. Signing.

- **Icon System** - SVG styles. Dark & light modes. Accessible labeling.

- **Photography Style** - Mood. Filter and effects. Subject.

#### Notes:

Embedded images and media make sites interesting.

But there are performance trade-offs, accessibility barriers, and the potential for information fragmentation to consider.

We should ask: Does this media supplement the written text, or are we filling because of poor layout planning?

---

## Slide 8: Web Components

### Using the power of website conventions



- **Interactive Elements** - Button styles and states. Navigation. Card components. Modals. Tooltips. Details and summary.

- **Animation Guidelines** - Transition speeds. Easing functions. User preferences.

- **Responsive Behaviour** - How elements adapt across devices. Media vs Container queries.

- **Spacing System** - Margin and padding fluid scaling.

#### Notes:

The web is interactive.

We need to tab, mouse-click, or finger-tap links and buttons, and we should get visual or audio feedback on our actions.

Visitors need to understand our UI and conventions are our friends in this aim.

Unfortunately, design systems have a history of breaking conventions:
1. Removing link underlines (Twitter Bootstrap V3).
2. Replacing form input titles with placeholders (Google's Material Design system).

We should avoid complexity and follow WCAG guidelines rather than blindly trust third parties even if they are used by millions.

---

## Slide 9: SEO & Discoverability

### Getting found and established with a SEO-first approach



- **Technical SEO** - Structured data/schema. Page speed targets. Mobile-first indexing considerations.

- **Content Strategy** - Publishing frequency. Site structure and internal linking.

- **Local SEO** - Google Business Profile. Geolocation tagging. EEAT. QR code.

#### Notes:

With well-structured semantic HTML content, SEO largely takes care of itself.

There can be gains from exploiting weaknesses in search engines, but it's risky.

For most businesses, their domain name (and the online authority it gains over time) is too valuable to throw away on tricks for short-term gains.

---

## Slide 10: Developer Specifications

### Code standards and policies on the tech used

- **Code Standards** - CSS methodology. Browser support. Accessibility requirements.

- **Performance** - Max page weight. Core Web Vitals targets. Image optimization standards.

- **Maintenance Protocols** - CMS guidelines. Dependency. Update schedule. Backup procedures. Security best practices.

- **Hosting & Infrastructure** - Environment specs, CDN usage, caching strategy.

---

## Slide 11: Applications

### How the brand is used across platforms

- **Social Media** - Profile images, cover photos, post templates (per platform)

- **Google Business Profile** - Photo requirements, post formatting.

- **Email** - Template structure, mobile optimization

- **Directory and Review Listings** - Profile images, cover photos, post templates (per platform)

- **Off-Line** - Community & Sponsorship. Print & Physical Materials

---

## Slide 12: Testing & Tracking

### How do we ethically measure success?



- **Our Privacy Policy** - Cookie stance. Use of dark patterns. Hidden tracking "pixels". Session replay tools.

- **Analytics Tools** - If needed, do we use privacy-first, no cookies tools like Plausible Analytics or Google Analytics where a visitor consent pop-up is required.

- **User Testing** - Can visitors find what they need. Are they confused about what is clickable. What is the impression of the site?

---

## Slide 13: Governance

### Who is responsible for what



- **Who can update what?** - Level of permissions to any CMS, Github or GitLab. Access to the Website Owner's Guide.

- **Review Schedule** - Quarterly audits, annual strategic reviews.

- **Decision Framework** - How to handle edge cases not covered in guidelines. What happens when someone is hit by a bus?

- **Change Log** - Tracking what and how?

- **Legal** - Is the website following the law and who follows changes?
