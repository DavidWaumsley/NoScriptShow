## Agile Development

An agile approach treats a website as a living product, not a one-off deliverable. It emphasises starting small, learning from real-world use, and iterating based on feedback and changing conditions.

- Deliver improvements in small increments instead of big launches.
- Start with a Minimum Viable Product rather than a “perfect” first version.
- Use feedback to guide rather than sticking rigidly to initial assumptions.

It’s a mindset more than a specific process. It shapes how we plan, decide, and work together over the whole life of the site.

## The Historical Evolution

Agile emerged in response to the rigid, plan-heavy software delivery models that struggled with change, commonly described as “waterfall.”

- In the 1990s, methods like Scrum and Extreme Programming explored faster, iterative ways of working.
- In 2001, 17 practitioners met in Snowbird, Utah and wrote the Agile Manifesto.
- Since then, Agile has spread beyond software into digital products, website projects, and many unrelated industries.

## Why It Matters

We prioritise outcomes, what works for users and the business, over outputs, how many pages or features we ship.

The web, our audience, and our business context will all keep changing. An agile approach helps a brand stay responsive, reduce risk, and focus effort where it makes a difference. Working in an agile way means:

- We accept that some early ideas will be wrong and treat that as learning, not failure.
- We plan with intention, but leave room to adapt when evidence or circumstances change.

## Core Principles

- People and collaboration over process and tools: Clear communication and shared understanding beat rigid workflows.
- Working website over exhaustive documentation: We favour getting a usable version live, then improving it, rather than over-planning.
- Responding to change over following a fixed plan: We treat the plan as a guide, not a contract, and adjust it when reality disagrees.
- User value over internal preference: We prioritise the features and content that make a real difference to our visitors.

This borrows from the spirit of the Agile Manifesto and applies it to web design and development.

## How Website Owners Apply This

- Define a MVP: the smallest set of pages and features that delivers genuine value.
- Work in short cycles with a “plan → build → review → adjust” rhythm.
- Regularly review analytics, feedback, and priorities, and update the backlog accordingly.
- Limit work: finish and launch small improvements rather than starting many things at once.
- Capture ideas and “nice-to-haves” in a backlog instead of trying to do everything in the first release.
- Remove or change features that are unused, confusing, or no longer aligned with goals.
